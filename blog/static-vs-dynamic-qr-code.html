Static vs Dynamic QR Codes: Key Differences (2025)

Published May 10, 2025  ·  7 min read  ·  Free QR Code Generator

When you start researching QR codes, you'll quickly run into two terms: static QR codes and dynamic QR codes. Many paid tools push dynamic codes as the only "professional" option — but that's not always true. This guide breaks down exactly what each type does, what the real trade-offs are, and which one you need for your use case.

Short answer: Static QR codes are free, permanent, and work for most use cases. Dynamic QR codes cost money, let you edit the destination URL, and add scan analytics — but most individuals and small businesses don't need them.

What Is a Static QR Code?

A static QR code encodes data directly inside the code itself. The URL, text, WiFi password, or UPI ID is physically embedded in the black-and-white pattern. Once generated, it cannot be changed — the pattern is the data.

Static codes:

  • Work forever — no server required, no subscription needed
  • Can encode any data type: URLs, plain text, WiFi credentials, vCards, UPI IDs, email addresses, and more
  • Scan exactly the same in any QR code reader, anywhere in the world
  • Can be generated for free (like on this site)
  • Do not track how many times they are scanned

What Is a Dynamic QR Code?

A dynamic QR code encodes a short redirect URL (e.g. qr.example.com/abc123) that points to a server. When someone scans it, their phone first hits that server, which then redirects them to the real destination. This indirection is what makes it "dynamic."

Dynamic codes:

  • Let you change the destination URL without reprinting the code
  • Provide scan analytics: total scans, scan location, device type, time of day
  • Require a paid subscription to the QR platform to keep working
  • Stop working if your subscription lapses or the company shuts down
  • Introduce a server dependency — one extra redirect adds latency and a point of failure
Risk: Dynamic QR codes require an active subscription. If you cancel your plan or the service goes offline, every QR code you printed — on menus, business cards, packaging — immediately stops working.

The "Dynamic Codes Are More Professional" Myth

Some QR platforms market dynamic codes as the only professional option. This is misleading. The type of QR code has nothing to do with how professional it looks — both types scan identically and look the same to the end user.

What actually makes a QR code look professional:

  • Adding your logo in the center of the code
  • Using your brand colours
  • Downloading at high resolution (300+ DPI for print)
  • Placing it at the right size (minimum 2×2 cm for printing)

Do Static QR Codes Expire?

No — a static QR code never expires on its own. The code is just data encoded in a pattern. It will scan correctly for as long as the physical print (or image) remains legible.

However, the destination can change:

  • If you encoded a URL and that webpage is taken down, the QR still scans — it just shows a 404 error
  • If you encoded a UPI ID or WiFi password and those change, you'll need to reprint the QR code

This is the one genuine advantage of dynamic codes: you can update the destination without reprinting. For most use cases though, the destination doesn't change, so this is a non-issue.

Are Dynamic QR Codes Worth the Price?

Only if you meet all three of these criteria:

  1. You're using QR codes in printed materials that are expensive or impossible to reprint
  2. You expect the destination URL to change in the future
  3. You need scan analytics (volume, location, device breakdown)

If you don't tick all three boxes, a free static QR code from this site will serve you equally well — without the monthly fee, subscription risk, or privacy trade-off.

Static QR Codes and the QR Code Types That Dynamic Can't Do

Here's something most dynamic QR platforms don't tell you: dynamic codes only support URL redirects. They can't natively encode WiFi credentials, UPI IDs, vCard contacts, email addresses, or SMS messages. For those use cases, you always need a static QR code.

This means if your goal is to share your WiFi password, collect a payment, or share your contact details — a dynamic code isn't even an option. Static is the only path.

Summary: Static QR codes are free, permanent, and support all data types. Dynamic codes cost money and are only worth it for printed campaigns with changing URLs and a need for scan analytics. For WiFi, UPI, vCard, and most link sharing — go static.
